General Information

What Are Locusts?

Locusts are large insects (Class Insecta) that belong to the Order Orthoptera, which houses similar creatures including grasshoppers and crickets.



Physical Characteristics

Locusts are invertebretes, herbivores, and look very similar to grasshoppers with their big hind legs. They are about 0.5-3 inches tall (about 1 paper clip) and weight roughly 0.07 ounces (about 2 paper clips).

Behavior/Life Cycle

Locusts experience two behaviors, solitary and gregarious, and can shift from one behavior to another. On hatching, a locust emerges wingless as a nonflying nymph, which can be either solitary or gregarious. A nymph can also change between behavior phases before becoming a flying adult after 24 to 95 days.

Location

Locusts and by extention swarms are typically found in Northern Africa, most of South Eurasia, East Indies, Middle East, Australia and New Zealand. It even has appeared in the USA sometimes.

Locusts vs Grasshoppers

Grasshoppers live completely solitary lives while locusts can change and live in swarms. Depending on the season, locusts can either be larger/smaller and stronger/weaker than grasshoppers.

Locust Swarms

One swarm can have around 80 million locusts, with a swarm of this size being able to eat over 400 million pounds of plants in a single day. Locust swarms are also able to travel great distances. One swarm in the 1950s flew all the way from northwest Africa to Great Britain.

Why Locusts Are A Problem

Locust swarms are extremely devastating by destorying agriculture and devouring crops. For example, the desert locust can at times affect approximately 20% of the world’s land surface.

How Do The Swarms Happen?

During dry seasons with low precipitation, locusts (solitary) huddle in packs in areas with vegetation. When rain comes, they produce rapidly become more crowded, shifting their behavior to gregarious. Not only can even change color and body shape when they move into this phase, but thheir endurance increases and their brains get larger.



Can We Prevent The Swarms?

It's very difficult to manage swarms of locusts due to many reasons including the remoteness and breadth of the areas across which they’re spread and limited resources in some of the affected countries. Some experts worry that locust plagues will worsen in a warming world. Rising sea temperatures are causing prolonged bouts of wet weather, including a surge of rare cyclones in eastern Africa and the Arabian Peninsula where desert locusts thrive.
